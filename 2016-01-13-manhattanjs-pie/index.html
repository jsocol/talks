History of Pie

Maybe a look at the history of pie will help.

4-6,000 years old

The original forerunners of pie were around almost as soon as we had agriculture.
Long journeys, especially by sea, required preserving food. Baking grain flour and water was an early method. If you've never hard "hard tack," you're really not missing out.
4,000 years ago, there's a Sumerian recipe for chicken pie.
There were pastry (dough with fat, more later) recipes around in antiquity.
By c 3,500ya, Egyptians were baking sweet galette-like pastries with honey filling.
For a very long time, this is what food was.
Hard "dough", local flour, salt and water.
Baking vessel, storage, serving. Local grains/bakers.
Meats and sauces, mostly.
By the middle ages, pies, known as "coffyns", the word for "box" were thick-sided, with no extra dish. Rich folks ate the filling, maybe the poor ate the sauce-soaked grain.
The English word "pie" seems, maybe to come from the word "magpie," maybe. No one really knows. Magpies collect things, pies were filled with whatever.
Pies came to the new world with settlers like the pilgrims.
Europeans had been making gourd pies for a long time. When they discovered the pumpkin, they started using it immediately.
To make the leap from a coffyn to a modern pie, we need pastry. Pastry was known in antiquity but returns in cookbooks around the 16th century.
These thin pastry crusts give us recognizable, modern pie.

Gluten

The structure of pastry—and bread—comes from gluten. Gluten is a complex of two proteins, gliadin and glutenin.
Gluten forms when these two proteins come into contact with water and are mixed together, usually by kneading.
Different amounts of water, kneading, and flour protein change how structural the dough will be.

What is pastry?

  1. Flour (gliadin and glutenin)
  2. Fat
  3. Water
What you're doing is creating little balls of fat coated in flour—gliadin and glutenin.
Then when you add water, and knead it a little (not too much) you cause gluten to form on the outside of the balls.
When you roll it out, you get sheets of gluten layered between sheets of fat.
These layers are "flaky" pie crust!
What about vodka? Or cold water? Or frozen butter?
"Cold" stuff is all about working the dough. As it warms up, it sticks to things.
Vodka has less water, so means less gluten forms.
Using a food processor, blend 70%-ish of the flour and all the butter. Incorporates some of the dry flour into the butter.
Then add remaining dry flour and water, pulse a little, and knead (a little) by hand. Stick in the fridge. Creates layers of dry, non-glutenated flour between layers of fat between layers of gluten. More flaky!

Fillings

Don't really have time, but ask me. Or if I have time, I'll just ramble for a minute. Fruit pies, pre-bake, use arrowroot. Custard pies, don't pre-bake, let it soak into the crust.
Apples: Try Newton Pippin apples. Fuji, Ambrosia, Honeycrisp.
Don't roast pumpkins. Use libby's. Tastes more like "pumpkin." Lots of things can be called "pumpkin," including a lot of squashes
